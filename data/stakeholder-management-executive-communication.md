# Communicating with Executives: A PM's Playbook

---

## Overview

Executive communication is a distinct skill from general stakeholder management. Executives operate under extreme time pressure and must make decisions across many domains simultaneously. This document covers how PMs communicate upward effectively — in writing, in meetings, and during escalations.

---

## What Executives Need from PMs

Executives don't need exhaustive detail — they need:

1. **Clarity on the situation** — What is happening and why it matters
2. **A clear recommendation** — What you think should be done and why
3. **The key risks** — What could go wrong and how it's being managed
4. **What you need from them** — Decision, resource, air cover, or just awareness

If your communication doesn't address these four things, you'll lose the room before you finish.

---

## The Minto Pyramid Principle

Barbara Minto's pyramid: lead with the answer, then support it.

**Wrong structure:**
> "We analyzed user data, ran three experiments, reviewed competitor approaches, interviewed 20 customers, and after all of this we concluded that we should invest in mobile."

**Right structure:**
> "We recommend investing in mobile next quarter. Three signals drove this: mobile accounts for 60% of signups but <5% of revenue, two enterprise deals were lost due to lack of mobile, and our top competitor launched a strong mobile product last month. Here's what investment looks like..."

Lead with the **So What**, not the **How We Got There**.

## Written Communication: The One-Pager

For major decisions, write a one-pager before any meeting. Structure:

```
Title: [Decision / Recommendation]

Situation:     What is the current state? (2-3 sentences)
Complication:  Why does it require a decision now? (1-2 sentences)
Recommendation: What should we do? (1 sentence + brief rationale)
Options considered: What else was considered and why ruled out?
Risks: Top 2-3 risks and mitigations
Ask: What do you need from this audience?
```

The one-pager forces clarity. If you can't write it in one page, your thinking isn't clear enough yet.

---

## In-Meeting Communication

**Before the meeting:**
- Send the one-pager 24 hours ahead. Don't present new information cold.
- Align with your manager and key stakeholders before the exec meeting — no surprises.
- Know your ask. Is this a decision meeting, an update, or an escalation?

**During the meeting:**
- State the ask in the first 60 seconds: "I need a decision on X today."
- Watch for cues that the room wants to go deeper on a specific point — follow that thread.
- If challenged, don't get defensive. "That's a fair concern. Here's how we're thinking about it..."
- If you don't know, say so: "I don't have that data with me. I'll follow up by EOD."

**After the meeting:**
- Send written notes within 24 hours: decisions made, open questions, follow-ups with owners.
- Never let a verbal commitment disappear into the ether.

---

## Escalation Communication

Escalations fail when they feel like complaints. Frame them as decision requests:

❌ "Engineering won't prioritize my feature and it's blocking my roadmap."

✅ "I need a decision on priority trade-off between [Feature A] and [Feature B]. Here's the business case for each, the team's current constraint, and my recommendation. I need leadership alignment to unblock the team."

Bring:
- The context (brief)
- The options (with trade-offs)
- Your recommendation
- The explicit ask

---

## Calibrating Detail Level

A common PM failure: providing analyst-level detail to executive audiences.

| Audience | Detail Level | Communication Mode |
|---|---|---|
| Individual engineer | Maximum detail, exact specs | Sync + written spec |
| Engineering lead | Implementation options + trade-offs | Weekly 1:1 |
| Director / VP | Outcome focus, key risks | Monthly review |
| C-suite | Strategic alignment + asks | One-pager + brief meeting |

When in doubt, ask: *What decision does this person need to make, and what's the minimum information for that decision?*

---

## Building Credibility Over Time

- **Be right more than you're wrong.** Make explicit predictions and track them.
- **Surface bad news early.** Executives forgive problems; they don't forgive surprises.
- **Own failures cleanly.** "Here's what happened, what I missed, and what I'm changing."
- **Follow through on commitments.** Nothing builds trust faster than doing exactly what you said.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Lead with your recommendation, not your analysis — use the Minto Pyramid.
- Write a one-pager for any significant decision before the meeting.
- Frame escalations as decision requests with options and a recommendation.
- Calibrate detail level to the audience's decision-making needs.
- Build credibility by being predictable, transparent, and reliable.
