# Translating Product Vision into Executable Strategy

**Author:** Shreya Nair  
**Source:** https://www.svpg.com/product-vision-vs-strategy/

---

## Overview

A product vision describes the future you're trying to create. Product strategy is the plan that bridges where you are today to that future. Many teams confuse the two — or skip strategy entirely and jump straight to a roadmap. This document explains how to build a coherent strategy layer between vision and execution.

---

## Why Strategy Is the Missing Middle

Most PMs operate between two extremes:

- **Vision** – "We want to be the world's most trusted financial assistant."
- **Roadmap** – "Q2: add bill-pay, Q3: credit score monitoring."

Without strategy, the roadmap is just a list of guesses. Strategy answers: *Why these bets? Why now? What must be true for them to work?*

---

## The Three Components of Product Strategy

### 1. Diagnosis
Clearly articulate the challenge. What's the core problem you're solving — for the customer and for the business?

> Example: "Our activation rate is 40%. Users sign up but don't reach the 'aha moment' within 7 days, so they churn before they see value."

### 2. Guiding Policy
What approach will you take to address the diagnosis? This rules out as much as it rules in.

> Example: "We will focus on reducing time-to-value for new users rather than acquiring more users until activation improves."

### 3. Coherent Actions
The specific bets — features, experiments, partnerships, pricing changes — that follow from the guiding policy.

---

## Common Strategy Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | What It Looks Like | Why It Fails |
|---|---|---|
| Feature factory | Roadmap driven by sales requests | No compounding value; teams are always reactive |
| Boiling the ocean | Trying to solve everything at once | No focus; resources spread too thin |
| Proxy metrics as strategy | "Grow DAU 20%" | Doesn't explain *how* to grow DAU |
| Competitor copycat | "They launched X, so we should too" | Plays to competitor strengths, not yours |

## Strategy Horizon Model

Think in three time horizons:

- **Horizon 1 (0–6 months):** Defend and optimize the core. Improve retention, fix key friction.
- **Horizon 2 (6–18 months):** Extend the core. Adjacent use cases, new user segments.
- **Horizon 3 (18–36 months):** Transform. New business models, platform bets.

Most teams over-invest in Horizon 1 and ignore Horizon 3 until it's too late.

---

## Writing a One-Page Strategy

A useful product strategy document answers five questions:

1. What is the target customer segment and unmet need?
2. What is the insight that gives you an advantage others lack?
3. What must be true for this strategy to succeed? (Key assumptions)
4. What are you explicitly *not* doing?
5. How will you know it's working? (Leading indicators)

---

## Key Takeaways

- Strategy is the *why* behind the roadmap, not the roadmap itself.
- Good strategy says no to more than it says yes to.
- Revisit strategy quarterly — markets shift and assumptions expire.
- A strategy written once and never shared is not a strategy; it must live in team decisions.
