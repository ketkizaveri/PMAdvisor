# Continuous Product Discovery: A Practical Guide

---

## Overview

Continuous discovery is the practice of making customer research a regular, weekly habit rather than a periodic project. Teams that practice continuous discovery make better decisions faster, with less risk. This document covers the habits, structures, and tools of continuous discovery.

---

## Why Discovery Must Be Continuous

Traditional discovery was project-based: a research sprint before a major initiative, then months of building. This model has two fatal flaws:

1. **Markets move faster than project timelines.** By the time you finish building from a six-month-old discovery sprint, the customer context has changed.
2. **Batch discovery creates batch decisions.** Teams feel locked in to what they "discovered" and don't update course as they learn.

Continuous discovery means the team never stops learning — at least one touchpoint with a customer every week.

---

## The Habit Stack

Teresa Torres recommends three core habits:

### Habit 1: Weekly Customer Interviews
- At least one customer interview per week, per product team
- PMs, designers, and engineers attend together (not PMs alone)
- Focus on the customer's story, not feature validation
- "Walk me through the last time you tried to do X" beats "Would you use this feature?"

### Habit 2: Synthesizing Into an Opportunity Solution Tree
Map what you learn into an explicit tree:
```
Desired Outcome (metric)
  └── Opportunity (customer need/pain/desire)
        └── Solution (potential feature/experiment)
              └── Experiment (assumption test)
```

### Habit 3: Assumption Testing Before Building
Before committing to a solution, explicitly list:
- What must be true for this solution to work?
- Which assumption is riskiest?
- What's the smallest test we can run to learn?

## Running Effective Discovery Interviews

**Recruiting:**
- Build a continuous recruiting pipeline — don't start from zero each time
- Offer 30-minute slots, compensate ($20–50 gift card is sufficient)
- Mix current customers, churned customers, and target non-customers

**Interview structure (30 minutes):**
1. **Warm-up (5 min):** Context — who they are, what they do, how they use your product
2. **Story (20 min):** Walk me through a recent time you tried to [job to be done]. What triggered it? What did you do first? What was frustrating? What happened next?
3. **Wrap (5 min):** Is there anything I didn't ask that I should have?

**What to avoid:**
- Leading questions: "Do you agree that X is a problem?"
- Solution testing: "What do you think of this mockup?" (too early)
- Hypothetical questions: "Would you use X?" (hypotheticals lie)

## The Opportunity Solution Tree in Practice

The OST keeps the team from falling in love with solutions before validating the opportunity:

1. Start with your desired **outcome** (a measurable metric)
2. Mine interviews for **opportunities** — unmet needs, pain points, desires
3. For each opportunity, brainstorm multiple **solutions** (avoid converging too early)
4. For each solution, identify the key **assumptions** and design minimal tests

A common mistake: jumping from outcome directly to solution. The opportunity layer is where insight lives.

---

## Assumption Testing Methods

| Assumption Type | Example | Test Method |
|---|---|---|
| Desirability | "Users want this feature" | Fake door test, concept test |
| Usability | "Users can complete this flow" | Usability test with prototype |
| Feasibility | "We can build this in 2 weeks" | Technical spike |
| Viability | "This supports our business model" | Financial model, stakeholder check |

Start with the riskiest assumption — the one that, if wrong, makes the whole solution worthless.

---

## Structuring the Weekly Rhythm

| Day | Activity |
|---|---|
| Monday | Review opportunity solution tree; decide this week's focus |
| Tuesday–Wednesday | Run 1–2 customer interviews |
| Thursday | Team synthesis session; update OST with new insights |
| Friday | Design/prototype/test for next week's assumption tests |

---

## Key Takeaways

- Continuous discovery is a habit, not a project — make customer contact a weekly commitment.
- Always have the whole triad (PM, design, engineering) in customer conversations.
- The opportunity solution tree prevents jumping to solutions before validating the problem.
- Test your riskiest assumption first with the smallest possible experiment.
- Synthesize weekly; don't let insights accumulate without mapping to decisions.
