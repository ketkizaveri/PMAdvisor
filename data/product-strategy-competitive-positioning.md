# Competitive Positioning for Product Managers

**Author:** Marcus Webb  
**Source:** https://www.reforge.com/blog/competitive-positioning

---

## Overview

Competitive positioning is not a marketing exercise — it's a strategic discipline that shapes which features you build, which segments you target, and how you price. This document covers frameworks PMs can use to develop and maintain sharp competitive positioning.

---

## What Positioning Actually Is

Positioning answers the question: *In the mind of your target customer, what do you stand for — and against whom?*

April Dunford's framing is useful here: positioning has five components:

1. **Competitive alternatives** – What would customers use if you didn't exist?
2. **Unique attributes** – What do you have that alternatives don't?
3. **Value** – What does that uniqueness enable for the customer?
4. **Target customer** – Who cares most about that value?
5. **Market frame of reference** – What category do you compete in?

---

## The Positioning Map

A positioning map plots your product against competitors on two axes that matter to customers. Choose axes based on what customers actually buy on — not what you wish they bought on.

```
                High Reliability
                      |
    Complex  ---------+--------- Simple
                      |
                Low Reliability
```

Good axis pairs for B2B SaaS might be: **Ease of use vs. Power**, **Time-to-value vs. Configurability**, or **Price vs. Integration depth**.

## Identifying Your Moat

A moat is a defensible competitive advantage. Common moats in software:

| Moat Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Network effects | Product becomes more valuable as more users join | Slack, LinkedIn |
| Data advantages | Proprietary data improves the product over time | Waze, Duolingo |
| Switching costs | High cost (time, money, effort) to leave | Salesforce, SAP |
| Brand | Trust and recognition reduce evaluation costs | Stripe, Figma |
| Scale economies | Unit costs fall as volume increases | AWS, Google Ads |

As a PM, your job is to build features that *reinforce* the moat — not just ship functionality.

## Competitive Intelligence for PMs

Where to gather signal:

- **Win/loss interviews** – Talk to prospects who chose a competitor and customers who almost churned
- **G2 / Capterra reviews** – Mine competitor reviews for unmet needs
- **Job postings** – Competitors hiring for specific roles signals strategic investments
- **Sales call notes** – Your sales team hears objections you never see in analytics
- **Product changelogs** – Track competitor releases in a shared doc

---

## Positioning Traps

**Trap 1: Feature parity as strategy**
Chasing every competitor feature erodes your differentiation. If you're always catching up, you'll never lead.

**Trap 2: Positioning to the wrong segment**
A positioning that resonates with enterprise buyers may alienate SMB. Know exactly *who* you're positioning to.

**Trap 3: Static positioning**
Markets shift. Reassess positioning when a significant competitor enters, exits, or pivots — and when your own product meaningfully expands.

---

## A Positioning Review Cadence

- **Monthly:** Update competitive feature matrix and win/loss data
- **Quarterly:** Review positioning with product, sales, and marketing
- **Annually:** Full reassessment of market frame of reference and target segment

---

## Key Takeaways

- Positioning is the strategic foundation for roadmap decisions, not a tagline.
- Know your competitive alternatives — they define the frame customers use to evaluate you.
- Build features that compound your moat, not just match competitors.
- Validate positioning with real customers, not internal consensus.
