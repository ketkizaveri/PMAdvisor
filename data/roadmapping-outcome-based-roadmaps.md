# Outcome-Based Roadmaps: Moving Beyond Feature Lists

---

## Overview

Traditional roadmaps are lists of features ordered by time. Outcome-based roadmaps replace features with business and customer outcomes — and let the team discover the best solutions to achieve them. This shift is one of the most impactful changes a PM can make to how their team operates.

---

## The Problem with Feature Roadmaps

Feature roadmaps create several dysfunctions:

- **False certainty:** Committing to a feature six months out assumes you already know the best solution — before you've done the discovery.
- **Output over outcome:** Teams celebrate shipping features, not solving problems.
- **Stakeholder theater:** Roadmaps become negotiation artifacts for internal politics, not genuine plans.
- **No learning loop:** When a feature ships and doesn't move the metric, there's no structured way to update the plan.

---

## What an Outcome-Based Roadmap Looks Like

Instead of:
```
Q1: Redesign onboarding flow
Q2: Add team collaboration features
Q3: Launch mobile app
```

An outcome-based roadmap might look like:
```
Q1: Increase 7-day activation rate from 40% → 60%
Q2: Increase team seat expansion from 1.2 → 1.8 seats per account
Q3: Reduce support ticket volume for onboarding by 30%
```

The *how* is left to the team to discover through continuous product discovery.

---

## How to Set Outcomes

Outcomes should be:

1. **Measurable** – You can track them with real data
2. **Meaningful** – They connect to business goals or customer value
3. **Achievable** – The team has meaningful influence over the metric
4. **Time-bound** – Has a clear review horizon (quarter, half-year)

Use the **opportunity solution tree** to connect outcomes to opportunities to solutions:

```
Outcome: Increase activation rate
  └── Opportunity: Users don't understand what to do on day 1
        └── Solution A: Interactive product tour
        └── Solution B: Personalized onboarding checklist
        └── Solution C: Proactive in-app messaging
```

---

## Getting Stakeholder Buy-In

The biggest challenge with outcome-based roadmaps is internal. Stakeholders often want to see specific features on a timeline.

**Strategies:**

- **Connect outcomes to company OKRs** — Show how the outcome supports what leadership already committed to.
- **Share the discovery process** — Bring stakeholders into weekly discovery reviews so they see solutions being explored, not just shipped.
- **Create a "now / next / later" view** — For stakeholders who need rough timelines, show current focus, next bets, and future horizon without hard dates.

---

## Now / Next / Later Format

This is the most practical outcome-based roadmap format for most teams:

| Horizon | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Now** | What we're actively working on this quarter | Activation rate improvement |
| **Next** | What we'll likely tackle next (subject to learning) | Expansion loop optimization |
| **Later** | Strategic bets we're considering | Mobile-first experience |

---

## Metrics to Govern the Roadmap

Each outcome should be paired with:

- **Primary metric:** The number you're trying to move
- **Counter metric:** What you're protecting (e.g., don't tank retention while improving activation)
- **Leading indicator:** An early signal that shows you're on track before the lag metric moves

---

## Key Takeaways

- Commit to outcomes, not features — keep the solution space open as long as possible.
- Use the opportunity solution tree to stay connected from metric to experiments.
- Bring stakeholders along via discovery — not just quarterly roadmap reviews.
- Review and update outcomes when evidence demands it; a roadmap is a living document.
