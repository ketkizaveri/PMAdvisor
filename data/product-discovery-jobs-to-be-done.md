# Jobs to Be Done: A PM's Framework for Understanding Customer Needs

---

## Overview

Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) is a framework for understanding why customers buy and use products. The core insight: customers don't buy products — they hire them to get a job done. When you understand the job, you understand what the customer really needs and who your true competitors are.

---

## The Core Idea

Clayton Christensen's milkshake story is the canonical example:

> A fast food chain wanted to increase milkshake sales. Traditional research (demographics, taste tests) was unhelpful. Researchers observed actual behavior and found that nearly half of milkshakes were purchased before 9 AM by commuters eating alone, who drove off immediately. The job: *keep me occupied and not hungry during my long morning commute.* The competitors: bananas, bagels, a cup of coffee.

Understanding the job revealed the actual use case, the actual buyer segment, and the actual competitors — none of which traditional segmentation would have found.

---

## Anatomy of a Job

A well-formed job statement has three parts:

**[Situation] + [Motivation] + [Expected Outcome]**

> "When I'm **onboarding a new enterprise customer** (situation), I want to **get them set up and seeing value quickly** (motivation), so I can **reduce the risk of churn before our first renewal** (expected outcome)."

Avoid vague job statements like "manage my workflow" or "communicate with my team" — these are too broad to be useful.

## The Forces of Progress

When a customer "fires" their old solution and "hires" yours, four forces are at play:

```
                DEMAND
                
Push of the ──────────────────► Pull of new
current situation               solution promise

◄──────────────────────────────────────────────
    Anxiety about         Attachment to
    new solution          current approach

                RESISTANCE
```

- **Push:** The frustrations with the current solution that make the customer open to switching
- **Pull:** The appeal of your product's promise
- **Anxiety:** The fear that the new solution won't work as promised
- **Inertia:** The comfort with the known, even if imperfect

Good product decisions reduce anxiety and inertia, not just increase pull.

## JTBD vs. Personas

Traditional personas describe *who* the customer is (age, role, personality). JTBD describes *what* they're trying to do.

| Dimension | Persona | JTBD |
|---|---|---|
| Focus | Demographics, attributes | Situation, goal, outcome |
| Value | Empathy, communication | Discovery, prioritization |
| Risk | Can become stereotypes | Requires rigorous research |
| Best for | Marketing, onboarding tone | Feature strategy, competitive analysis |

The best teams use both: personas for communication, JTBD for discovery.

## Uncovering Jobs Through Research

**Effective JTBD interview questions:**

- "Take me back to the moment you decided to look for a solution to this. What was happening?"
- "What were you using before? Why wasn't it working anymore?"
- "When did you first realize you had this problem?"
- "What would you do if this product disappeared tomorrow?"
- "Who else was affected by this decision?"

What you're looking for: **the triggering event** that initiated the buying process, and **the emotional and functional goals** the customer was trying to achieve.

---

## Jobs at Different Levels

Jobs exist at different abstraction levels:

| Level | Example |
|---|---|
| **Big hire** | Switching to a new product category | Replacing spreadsheets with a dedicated tool |
| **Little hire** | Opening your app in a specific moment | Opening Spotify while driving |
| **Struggling moment** | The exact situation that triggers product use | "I need to pull data for an exec meeting in 20 minutes" |

Most product discovery should focus on **struggling moments** — they're specific enough to drive design decisions.

---

## Applying JTBD to Roadmap Decisions

For each feature candidate, ask:

1. What job is this feature hired to do?
2. Who has that job and how often do they have it?
3. How do they do it today? (Understand the current "hire")
4. What makes our version better at the job?
5. What anxieties might prevent adoption even if it's better?

Features that can't answer question 1 clearly should be deprioritized.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Customers hire products to get a job done — understand the job, not just the product usage.
- Use the forces model to understand what creates demand and what creates resistance.
- Write specific job statements with situation, motivation, and expected outcome.
- Interview customers about switching events — the triggering moment reveals the real job.
- JTBD and personas are complementary; use both for different purposes.
